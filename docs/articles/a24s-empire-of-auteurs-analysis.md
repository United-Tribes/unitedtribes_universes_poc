# A24's Empire of Auteurs

**Title:** A24's Empire of Auteurs
**Source:** The New Yorker (Onward and Upward with the Arts)
**Author:** Alex Barasch
**Publication Date:** August 25, 2025
**ID:** [audio article reading]
**Duration:** 52:13
**Type:** Long-Form Magazine Article (Spoken Audio Reading)

A New Yorker long-form profile by staff writer Alex Barasch on A24, the New York-based independent film and television studio that has spent thirteen years quietly turning small art-house pickups into Best Picture winners and is now testing whether it can scale up to mainstream blockbusters without losing the auteur-driven identity that built its fan base. The piece is structured around an extended embed with Noah Sacco, A24's head of film, and includes interviews with co-founders David Fenkel and Daniel Katz, head of distribution Nicolette Aizenberg, head of TV Ravi Nandan, and a deep bench of A24 filmmakers — Robert Eggers, Sofia Coppola, Ari Aster, Halina Reijn, Alex Garland, Celine Song, Greta Gerwig, Barry Jenkins, the Philippou twins, Eva Victor, Kelly Reichardt, Dwayne Johnson — alongside ancillary voices including Satanic Temple spokesperson Jex Blackmore (on the 2016 marketing rollout for The Witch) and former A24 Indie head David Laub (on the studio's drift away from the small films that built its reputation). The piece walks through A24's founding in 2012 by Fenkel, Katz, and John Hodges with $14 million in Guggenheim Partners seed funding, traces the rise from Spring Breakers and The Bling Ring through Moonlight, Lady Bird, Ex Machina, Hereditary, Midsommar, Uncut Gems, Past Lives, and Everything Everywhere All at Once, and ends on the present-tense question of what happens next: a $3.5 billion valuation backed by Thrive Capital (also a major OpenAI backer), an AI division called A24 Labs led by Scott Belsky, $70 million productions from Josh Safdie (Marty Supreme) and Benny Safdie (The Smashing Machine), Alex Garland's adaptation of FromSoftware's Elden Ring, and a Cannes 2025 slate that included Ari Aster's Eddington, Harry Lighton's Pillion, and Spike Lee's Highest 2 Lowest. The recurring tension across the article is whether a studio that built itself on niche provocation can hold onto its identity as it scales — whether the same instincts that produced Under the Skin and Moonlight in the first decade can now produce four-quadrant work in the second.
